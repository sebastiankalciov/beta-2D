People


John von Neumann

The von Neumann architecture—also known as the von Neumann model or Princeton architecture—is a computer architecture based on a 1945 description by John von Neumann, and by others, in the First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC. The document describes a design architecture for an electronic digital computer with these components:

  • A processing unit with both an arithmetic logic unit and processor registers
  • A control unit that includes an instruction register and a program counter
  • Memory that stores data and instructions
  • Input and output mechanisms

Gordon Moore

The observation that the number of transistors on computer chips doubles approximately every two years is known as Moore’s Law. Moore’s Law is not a law of nature, but an observation of a long-term trend in how technology is changing. The law was first described by Gordon E. Moore, the co-founder of Intel, in 1965.

Walter Brattain, John Bardeen, and William Shockley

The invention of the transistor marked a significant milestone in electronics, paving the way for modern electronic devices and earning the trio the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1956. Walter Brattain, John Bardeen, and William Shockley were the inventors of the transistor, a revolutionary solid-state device. A spokesman claimed that "it may have far-reaching significance in electronics and electrical communication." Despite its delicate mechanical construction, many thousands of units were produced in a metal cartridge package as the Bell Labs "Type A" transistor.

John Hannesy

A pioneer of reduced instruction set computing (RISC) architecture. He co-authored the influential textbook "Computer Architecture: A Quantitative Approach" with David Patterson and later served as the President of Stanford University.

Seymour Cray

Known as the "father of supercomputing," he founded Cray Research and designed a series of innovative supercomputers that set performance records for decades.
